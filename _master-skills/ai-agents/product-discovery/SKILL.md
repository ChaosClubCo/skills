# Product Discovery

## Overview

Product discovery is the systematic process of understanding customer problems, identifying valuable opportunities, and validating solutions before committing significant development resources. It encompasses research methods, analytical frameworks, and decision-making processes that increase the probability of building products customers actually need and will pay for.

Effective discovery separates assumption from evidence, opinion from insight. It combines qualitative research to understand why customers behave as they do with quantitative analysis to measure the scope and impact of opportunities. The best product teams treat discovery as a continuous practice, not a one-time phase, maintaining deep connection with customer reality while making faster, better-informed product decisions.

### Why This Matters

Discovery dramatically improves product success rates and resource efficiency. Teams practicing continuous discovery ship features that drive 2-3x higher adoption rates and significantly reduce wasted development effort on low-value features. The cost of discovery is a fraction of development cost, making it one of the highest-ROI activities in product development. Perhaps most importantly, discovery creates organizational confidence in product decisions.

## When to Use

### Primary Triggers

- **New Product Initiative**: Starting from scratch, defining the problem space
- **Feature Planning**: Validating assumptions before development commitment
- **Customer Feedback Surge**: High volume of requests requiring synthesis
- **Metrics Decline**: Usage or satisfaction dropping without clear cause
- **Market Entry**: Understanding new customer segment needs
- **Competitive Response**: Understanding why customers choose alternatives

### Specific Use Cases

| Scenario | Discovery Focus | Key Methods |
|----------|----------------|-------------|
| Startup early stage | Problem-solution fit | Customer interviews, JTBD |
| Feature validation | Solution desirability | Prototype testing, surveys |
| Retention problem | Churn driver analysis | Exit interviews, journey mapping |
| Growth opportunity | Expansion use cases | Usage analysis, upsell research |
| New market | Segment understanding | Ethnographic research, surveys |
| Usability issues | Experience problems | Usability testing, analytics |

### Discovery Intensity Levels

| Decision Risk | Discovery Depth | Timeline | Methods |
|---------------|-----------------|----------|---------|
| Low (minor feature) | Light | 1-2 weeks | Data analysis, quick tests |
| Medium (significant feature) | Standard | 2-4 weeks | Interviews, prototypes |
| High (major initiative) | Deep | 4-8 weeks | Full research program |
| Very High (new product) | Comprehensive | 8-16 weeks | Multi-method research |

## Core Processes

### Phase 1: Problem Space Exploration

#### Jobs-to-be-Done Framework

```markdown
## JTBD Analysis Framework

### Job Statement Structure
"When I [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."

### Job Layers
1. **Functional Job**: What the customer is trying to accomplish
2. **Emotional Job**: How they want to feel
3. **Social Job**: How they want to be perceived

### Job Map Template
| Stage | Customer Action | Desired Outcome | Pain Points |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Define | Determine what's needed | Clarity on requirements | Unclear criteria |
| Locate | Find options/resources | Complete information | Fragmented sources |
| Prepare | Get ready to execute | Confidence to proceed | Complexity barriers |
| Confirm | Validate approach | Assurance of success | Uncertainty |
| Execute | Perform the task | Efficient completion | Time/effort waste |
| Monitor | Track progress | Real-time visibility | Blind spots |
| Modify | Make adjustments | Flexibility to adapt | Rigidity |
| Conclude | Finish and transition | Clean completion | Loose ends |

### JTBD Interview Guide
**Opening**: "Tell me about the last time you [did X]..."

**Core Questions**:
1. What triggered you to look for a solution?
2. What did you hope to accomplish?
3. What alternatives did you consider?
4. What made you choose your approach?
5. What was frustrating about the experience?
6. What would ideal look like?

**Forces of Progress**:
- Push: What's making the current situation unacceptable?
- Pull: What's the attraction of a new solution?
- Anxiety: What concerns exist about changing?
- Habit: What's comfortable about the current state?
```

### Phase 2: Research Execution

#### Interview Program Design

```markdown
## Customer Interview Framework

### Interview Structure (60 min)
1. **Opening (5 min)**
   - Introduction and context
   - Permission to record
   - Confidentiality assurance

2. **Background (10 min)**
   - Role and responsibilities
   - Company context
   - Relevant experience

3. **Problem Exploration (25 min)**
   - Current workflow/process
   - Pain points and frustrations
   - Impact and consequences
   - Workarounds and adaptations

4. **Solution Discussion (15 min)**
   - Ideal solution characteristics
   - Concept/prototype feedback
   - Trade-off preferences

5. **Wrap-up (5 min)**
   - Summary and clarification
   - Follow-up permission
   - Referral requests

### Question Types and Techniques
| Type | Purpose | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Open-ended | Explore broadly | "Tell me about..." |
| Specific | Get details | "What happened next?" |
| Comparative | Understand preferences | "How does X compare to Y?" |
| Hypothetical | Test concepts | "If you could..." |
| Emotional | Understand feelings | "How did that make you feel?" |
| Quantitative | Scope problems | "How often does this happen?" |

### Interview Analysis Template
**Participant**: [ID/Name]
**Date**: [Date]
**Key Jobs**: [Jobs identified]
**Pain Points**: [Pains mentioned]
**Quotes**: [Verbatim quotes]
**Insights**: [Analyst observations]
**Follow-ups**: [Questions for next time]
```

## Common Pitfalls

### Discovery Anti-Patterns

1. **Confirmation Bias**: Seeking evidence for pre-determined conclusions
   - *Mitigation*: Diverse perspectives, devil's advocate

2. **Analysis Paralysis**: Never-ending research without decisions
   - *Mitigation*: Time-boxed discovery, decision checkpoints

3. **Solution Jumping**: Skipping problem understanding
   - *Mitigation*: Problem framing before solution discussion

4. **Sample Bias**: Talking only to accessible or friendly customers
   - *Mitigation*: Intentional sampling, diverse recruitment

5. **Discovery Theater**: Doing research without acting on it
   - *Mitigation*: Clear decision criteria, stakeholder commitment

6. **Feature Requests as Research**: Treating requests as validated needs
   - *Mitigation*: Explore underlying jobs and problems

7. **One-Shot Discovery**: Treating discovery as a phase, not practice
   - *Mitigation*: Continuous discovery rhythms
