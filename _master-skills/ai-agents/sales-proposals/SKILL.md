# Sales Proposals

## Overview

A sales proposal is the written argument that converts interest into revenue. It bridges the gap between what a prospect needs and what you deliver, quantifying value in terms the buyer cares about. The difference between proposals that win and proposals that lose is rarely the product -- it is the clarity of the value narrative, the credibility of the proof points, and the ease of saying yes.

This skill covers the full proposal lifecycle: discovery synthesis, executive summary writing, solution design, pricing construction, competitive positioning, ROI modeling, and RFP response management. It provides frameworks for structuring proposals that align to buyer psychology, reduce friction in the approval process, and systematically improve win rates over time.

### Why This Matters
- Proposals are the single most-reviewed artifact in B2B purchase decisions
- Structured proposals with ROI models close at 2-3x the rate of feature-focused pitches
- Pricing presentation format directly impacts perceived value and negotiation dynamics
- RFP responses with reusable content libraries reduce response time by 60-70%

## Core Workflow

1. **Synthesize Discovery Findings** -- Review all discovery notes, call recordings, and stakeholder inputs. Identify the primary business problem, quantified pain points, decision criteria, and evaluation timeline. Map each stakeholder's priorities.

2. **Define the Win Theme** -- Craft a single sentence that captures why your solution is uniquely positioned to solve this specific buyer's problem. Every section of the proposal should reinforce this theme.

3. **Draft the Executive Summary** -- Write 1-2 pages that a C-level reader can use to approve the deal without reading further. Include: problem statement, proposed solution, expected outcomes, investment summary, and recommended next step.

4. **Design the Solution Section** -- Map each buyer requirement to your solution capability. Use a needs-capabilities matrix. Include architecture diagrams, implementation timelines, and integration details as appropriate.

5. **Build the Pricing Section** -- Structure pricing to anchor on value, not cost. Lead with the total value delivered, present investment tiers, and frame pricing relative to the cost of inaction.

6. **Add Proof and Credibility** -- Include case studies, testimonials, certifications, and performance guarantees relevant to the buyer's industry and use case. Specificity beats volume.

7. **Create the Decision Framework** -- Provide a comparison matrix that positions your solution favorably against alternatives. Include evaluation criteria the buyer has stated, scored objectively.

8. **Polish and Quality-Check** -- Review for consistency, accuracy, brand alignment, and buyer-specific personalization. Verify all names, titles, company references, and financial figures.

9. **Package for Delivery** -- Format for the buyer's preferred medium (PDF, web link, presentation). Include a cover letter with a clear call to action and proposed next meeting date.

## Best Practices

### Proposal Writing Principles

1. **Lead with the buyer's problem, not your product** -- The first page should be about them, not you. Mirror their language from discovery calls.

2. **Quantify everything** -- Replace "improve efficiency" with "reduce processing time from 4 hours to 22 minutes." Vague value claims are invisible to decision-makers.

3. **Structure for skimmers** -- Use bold headings, bullet points, callout boxes, and summary tables. Assume 80% of readers will skim. Put the most critical information in the first sentence of every section.

4. **Anchor pricing on value** -- Always present the total value delivered before presenting the investment. The ratio between value and cost should feel obviously favorable.

5. **Include the cost of inaction** -- Frame the status quo as more expensive than the solution. Calculate what the buyer loses each month by not solving the problem.

6. **Personalize relentlessly** -- Generic proposals lose to personalized ones. Reference specific conversations, stakeholder names, company metrics, and industry challenges.

7. **Make the next step frictionless** -- End with a specific proposed action, date, and single point of contact. Never end with "let us know if you have questions."

### RFP Response Strategy

- **Read the entire RFP before writing anything** -- Identify evaluation criteria, scoring methodology, and mandatory requirements
- **Answer the question asked, not the question you wish was asked** -- Non-responsive answers are disqualifying
- **Use the exact language of the requirement in your response** -- Evaluators often use keyword matching
- **Lead each answer with compliance status** -- "Fully compliant. [Explanation follows]"
- **Build a reusable content library** -- Tag responses by topic, product area, and compliance category for future RFPs
- **Assign section owners and set internal deadlines 3 days before submission** -- Last-minute assembly destroys quality

### Competitive Positioning Rules

- Never name competitors negatively in writing; instead, describe category alternatives
- Focus on unique differentiators, not feature parity claims
- Use "unlike traditional approaches" rather than "unlike [Competitor Name]"
- Position weaknesses as deliberate design choices: "We chose to focus deeply on X rather than spreading thin across X and Y"
- Let case studies and third-party validations do the competitive arguing

## Common Patterns

### Pattern 1: Multi-Stakeholder Proposal

When the buying committee includes 3+ stakeholders with different priorities:

1. Write a shared executive summary that frames the solution around the business outcome all stakeholders agree on
2. Create stakeholder-specific appendix sections:
   - **For the CFO**: ROI model, payback period, TCO comparison, risk mitigation
   - **For the CTO/IT**: Architecture diagram, security posture, integration approach, SLA details
   - **For the End-User Champion**: Ease of use, training plan, change management, adoption timeline
3. Reference each stakeholder by name in their relevant section
4. Include a decision timeline that accounts for internal review cycles

### Pattern 2: Competitive Displacement Proposal

When replacing an incumbent vendor:

1. Acknowledge the buyer's existing investment and avoid disparaging the current vendor
2. Frame the switch as evolution, not replacement: "Building on what you've learned with [current approach]"
3. Include a detailed migration plan with risk mitigation steps
4. Offer a parallel-run period or pilot phase to reduce perceived risk
5. Calculate the total cost of switching (migration + training + productivity dip) and show net positive ROI within a defined timeframe
6. Provide references from clients who made the same switch

### Pattern 3: Land-and-Expand Pricing

When the goal is initial adoption with planned growth:

1. Offer a focused Phase 1 scope at a low-friction price point
2. Define clear success criteria that trigger Phase 2 expansion
3. Include contracted expansion pricing (locked rates for 12-24 months)
4. Show the Phase 1 ROI independently (it must stand alone)
5. Map the expansion roadmap to the buyer's stated growth plans

### Pattern 4: Emergency / Urgent Response

When the buyer has an immediate pain point and compressed timeline:

1. Lead with speed: "We can have you live in [X] days/weeks"
2. Propose a rapid deployment scope (MVP) followed by optimization phase
3. Include an implementation timeline with daily/weekly milestones
4. Offer dedicated onboarding resources for the accelerated period
5. Add a guarantee or SLA tied to the launch date
6. Price premium for urgency is acceptable if framed as dedicated resource allocation

## Output Formats

### Proposal Document Structure

```
COVER PAGE
- Prospect company name and logo
- Proposal title: "[Solution] for [Company]: [Value Outcome]"
- Prepared by / Prepared for with names and titles
- Date and validity period ("Valid through [date]")

TABLE OF CONTENTS

SECTION 1: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (1-2 pages)
- The challenge
- Proposed solution
- Expected outcomes with metrics
- Investment summary
- Recommended next step

SECTION 2: UNDERSTANDING YOUR NEEDS (2-3 pages)
- Business context and strategic priorities
- Current challenges (mirroring discovery findings)
- Requirements summary table

SECTION 3: PROPOSED SOLUTION (3-5 pages)
- Solution overview and architecture
- Needs-capabilities mapping
- Implementation approach and timeline
- Integration details

SECTION 4: PROOF OF VALUE (2-3 pages)
- Relevant case studies (2-3, industry-matched)
- Client testimonials
- Performance metrics and benchmarks
- Certifications and compliance

SECTION 5: INVESTMENT (1-2 pages)
- Pricing tiers or custom pricing
- ROI model
- Cost of inaction analysis
- Payment terms and options

SECTION 6: WHY [YOUR COMPANY] (1 page)
- Company overview
- Key differentiators
- Team bios (project leads)

SECTION 7: NEXT STEPS (0.5 page)
- Proposed decision timeline
- Specific next action with date
- Primary contact information

APPENDICES (as needed)
- Technical specifications
- Security and compliance documentation
- Full terms and conditions
- Stakeholder-specific deep dives
```
