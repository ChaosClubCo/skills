# Design Thinking

## Overview

Design thinking is a human-centered approach to innovation that draws from the designer's toolkit to integrate the needs of people, the possibilities of technology, and the requirements for business success. It provides a structured yet flexible methodology for tackling complex, ambiguous problems through empathy, creativity, and iterative experimentation.

The approach moves through five interconnected phases: Empathize (understand users deeply), Define (frame the right problem), Ideate (generate diverse solutions), Prototype (build to learn), and Test (validate with users). While presented sequentially, design thinking is inherently iterative, with teams cycling back through phases as learning emerges.

Design thinking democratizes innovation by providing tools that anyone can use. It shifts focus from solution-first thinking to problem-first exploration, dramatically increasing the likelihood of building products and services people actually want.

### Why This Matters

Traditional problem-solving often jumps to solutions before fully understanding problems, leading to wasted effort and poor outcomes. Design thinking's emphasis on empathy and iteration reduces the risk of building the wrong thing while accelerating learning. Organizations that master design thinking consistently outperform competitors in innovation and customer satisfaction.

## When to Use

### Primary Triggers

- Facing ambiguous or complex problems
- Developing new products or services
- Reimagining customer experiences
- Driving organizational innovation
- Breaking through creative blocks
- Aligning diverse stakeholder perspectives
- Entering new markets or segments
- Transforming existing offerings

### Specific Use Cases

| Scenario | Design Thinking Focus | Expected Outcome |
|----------|---------------------|------------------|
| New Product Development | Full 5-phase process | Validated product concept |
| Service Improvement | Empathy + Define + Ideate | Improvement roadmap |
| Problem Exploration | Empathy + Define | Problem statement |
| Solution Generation | Ideate phase focus | Concept alternatives |
| Concept Validation | Prototype + Test | Go/no-go decision |
| Team Alignment | Define phase workshop | Shared problem framing |

#### Empathy Interview Guide

```markdown
## Interview Structure

### Opening (5 min)
- Thank participant
- Explain purpose (learning, not selling)
- Confirm recording consent
- Set comfortable tone

### Background (10 min)
- Role and context
- Typical day/week
- Relevant history
- Goals and motivations

### Core Exploration (30-45 min)
- Walk me through last time you [did X]
- What were you trying to accomplish?
- What was hard about that?
- How did that make you feel?
- What did you do next?
- Show me how you [do task]

### Probing Techniques
- "Tell me more about that..."
- "Why is that important to you?"
- "What do you mean by [term]?"
- "How did that make you feel?"
- "What would ideal look like?"

### Closing (5 min)
- Anything else I should know?
- Who else should I talk to?
- May I follow up if questions arise?
- Thank you

## Interview Note-Taking

### Capture Categories
- QUOTES: Exact words (in quotation marks)
- BEHAVIORS: What they did (observable)
- EMOTIONS: How they felt (stated or observed)
- NEEDS: Underlying requirements
- INSIGHTS: Your interpretations (mark clearly)
```

### Phase 2: Define

#### Problem Framing Techniques

```markdown
## Problem Statement Formats

### "How Might We" (HMW)
Structure: How might we [verb] [user] [outcome]?

Good HMW:
- Specific enough to inspire solutions
- Broad enough to allow creativity
- Focused on user need, not solution

Examples:
- "How might we help first-time parents feel confident making feeding decisions?"
- "How might we reduce the anxiety small business owners feel about cash flow?"

Bad HMW:
- "How might we build an app?" (solution-focused)
- "How might we improve things?" (too vague)

### Point of View (POV) Statement
Structure: [User] needs [need] because [insight]

Example:
"Working parents juggling remote work need ways to maintain focus during important calls because unexpected interruptions from children create professional embarrassment and stress."

### Problem Statement
Structure: [Context] + [Current state] + [Gap] + [Desired state]

Example:
"In today's remote work environment, working parents frequently experience interruptions during video calls that cause professional embarrassment and meeting disruptions. They need a way to maintain professionalism while being available to their children in emergencies."
```

#### User Need Identification

```markdown
## Insight Synthesis Process

### Step 1: Download Research
- Post all observations on sticky notes
- One insight per note
- Include source reference

### Step 2: Cluster Themes
- Group related observations
- Name each cluster
- Look for patterns

### Step 3: Identify Tensions
- Find contradictions
- Note surprises
- Highlight extremes

### Step 4: Extract Needs
- What underlying needs drive behavior?
- What jobs are users trying to do?
- What outcomes do they seek?

### Step 5: Prioritize
- Which needs are most important?
- Which are underserved?
- Which align with business goals?

## Needs Classification

### Functional Needs
- Tasks to accomplish
- Problems to solve
- Goals to achieve

### Emotional Needs
- How they want to feel
- What they want to avoid feeling
- Identity and status

### Social Needs
- How they want to be perceived
- Belonging and connection
- Social validation
```

### Phase 3: Ideate

#### Ideation Session Design

```markdown
## Brainstorming Workshop Structure

### Preparation
- Clear HMW statement posted
- Diverse participants (5-8)
- Materials: sticky notes, markers, timer
- Rules posted visibly

### Rules of Brainstorming
1. Defer judgment
2. Encourage wild ideas
3. Build on others' ideas
4. Stay focused on topic
5. One conversation at a time
6. Be visual
7. Go for quantity

### Session Flow

#### Warm-Up (10 min)
- Energizer activity
- Share the HMW
- Review rules

#### Individual Ideation (10 min)
- Silent brainstorming
- One idea per sticky note
- Aim for 10+ ideas each

#### Share and Build (20 min)
- Post ideas on wall
- Quick explanation (10 sec each)
- Add building ideas

#### Structured Techniques (30 min)
- SCAMPER method
- Reverse brainstorming
- Analogous inspiration

#### Cluster and Vote (15 min)
- Group similar ideas
- Dot voting (3-5 votes each)
- Discuss top concepts

#### Concept Development (20 min)
- Select 2-3 top ideas
- Develop into concepts
- Prepare share-out
```

#### Ideation Techniques

```markdown
## Creative Thinking Methods

### SCAMPER
- Substitute: What else can be used?
- Combine: What can be merged?
- Adapt: What else is like this?
- Modify: What can be changed?
- Put to other use: Other applications?
- Eliminate: What can be removed?
- Reverse: What if opposite?

### Worst Possible Idea
1. Generate intentionally bad ideas
2. Analyze what makes them bad
3. Reverse to find good ideas
4. Removes fear of "wrong" answers

### Analogous Inspiration
1. Identify the core challenge
2. Find parallel domains
3. Study how they solve it
4. Translate back to your context

Example: "How do emergency rooms handle triage?"
Applied to: Customer support prioritization

### Constraint Storming
1. Add artificial constraints
2. Generate ideas within constraints
3. Remove constraints, keep useful ideas

Constraints to try:
- "What if we had only $100?"
- "What if it had to work in 5 seconds?"
- "What if users couldn't read?"
- "What if we had unlimited budget?"

### Role Storming
- Generate ideas as someone else
- "What would [Elon Musk] do?"
- "How would a 5-year-old solve this?"
- Breaks personal creative blocks
```

- Final validation

#### Rapid Prototyping Methods

```markdown
## Prototyping Toolkit

### Paper Prototyping (Physical)
Materials: Paper, markers, scissors, tape
Time: 30-60 minutes
Best for: Mobile apps, simple interfaces

Process:
1. Sketch main screens
2. Create interaction elements
3. Simulate transitions manually
4. Observe user interactions

### Digital Wireframes
Tools: Balsamiq, Figma, Sketch
Time: 2-4 hours
Best for: Web applications, complex flows

Process:
1. Map key screens
2. Build wireframes
3. Link interactions
4. Add basic content

### Storyboarding
Materials: Template or blank paper
Time: 1-2 hours
Best for: Service experiences, journeys

Process:
1. Define key moments
2. Sketch each frame
3. Add captions/dialogue
4. Walk through narrative

### Role Playing
Materials: Props, script outline
Time: 30-60 minutes
Best for: Service interactions, conversations

Process:
1. Define scenario
2. Assign roles
3. Act out experience
4. Debrief and iterate

### Physical Mockups
Materials: Cardboard, foam, 3D printing
Time: 2-8 hours
Best for: Physical products, hardware

Process:
1. Define dimensions
2. Build rough model
3. Test in context
4. Iterate form factor
```

### Phase 5: Test

#### User Testing Protocol

```markdown
## Testing Session Structure

### Preparation
- Define learning goals (3-5 specific questions)
- Recruit appropriate participants
- Prepare prototype and materials
- Create task scenarios

### Session Agenda (60 min typical)

#### Setup (5 min)
- Welcome and comfort
- Explain think-aloud protocol
- Confirm consent

#### Context Questions (10 min)
- Background and experience
- Current behaviors
- Relevant needs

#### Task-Based Testing (30 min)
- Present scenario (not solution)
- Observe task completion
- Note behaviors and comments
- Ask clarifying questions

#### Debrief (10 min)
- Overall impressions
- Likes and dislikes
- Comparison to current solution
- Suggestions for improvement

#### Wrap-Up (5 min)
- Thank participant
- Explain next steps
- Provide compensation

## Observer Guidelines
- Don't help or lead
- Note quotes verbatim
- Track behaviors and emotions
- Save questions for the end
- Look for surprises
```

#### Test Analysis and Iteration

```markdown
## Testing Debrief Process

### Immediate Capture (End of Day)
- Each observer shares top 3 observations
- Cluster into themes
- Identify critical issues
- Note positive validations

### Pattern Recognition
| Finding | Participants | Severity | Action |
|---------|-------------|----------|--------|
| [Issue] | 4/5 | High | Fix now |
| [Issue] | 2/5 | Medium | Fix next |
| [Issue] | 1/5 | Low | Monitor |

### Iteration Planning
1. What must change immediately?
2. What should we explore further?
3. What is validated and should keep?
4. What new questions emerged?

### Deciding When to Iterate vs. Proceed
Iterate if:
- Critical usability issues found
- Core value proposition unclear
- Major negative reactions

Proceed if:
- Issues are minor/cosmetic
- Core concept validated
- Clear path to fix in development
```

## Common Pitfalls

### Design Thinking Mistakes

1. **Skipping Empathy**
   - Problem: Jumping to solutions without understanding users
   - Impact: Building wrong thing
   - *Mitigation*: Mandate minimum research before ideation

2. **Confirmation Bias in Research**
   - Problem: Hearing what you want to hear
   - Impact: Invalid insights
   - *Mitigation*: Diverse interviewers, structured analysis

3. **Falling in Love with First Idea**
   - Problem: Insufficient exploration of alternatives
   - Impact: Suboptimal solution
   - *Mitigation*: Force generation of multiple concepts

4. **Over-Polished Prototypes**
   - Problem: Too much investment before validation
   - Impact: Resistance to change
   - *Mitigation*: Set fidelity limits, time-box prototyping

5. **Testing with Wrong Users**
   - Problem: Feedback from non-target audience
   - Impact: Misleading results
   - *Mitigation*: Strict screener criteria

6. **One-and-Done Mentality**
   - Problem: Single iteration treated as final
   - Impact: Unrealized potential
   - *Mitigation*: Plan for multiple cycles

7. **Theater Without Action**
   - Problem: Workshops that don't lead to implementation
   - Impact: Wasted effort, cynicism
   - *Mitigation*: Clear ownership of next steps
