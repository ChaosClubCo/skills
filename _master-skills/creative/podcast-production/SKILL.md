# Podcast Production

> Craft compelling audio narratives that captivate listeners from cold open to closing credits.

## Description

This skill covers the full podcast production lifecycle, from concept development and episode planning through recording, editing, mixing, and distribution. It provides frameworks for narrative structure, audio composition, sound design, and technical editing workflows. The skill addresses show identity, episode cadence, guest management, and audience growth strategies. It also covers show notes, transcription, and cross-platform distribution to maximize reach and discoverability.

## Instructions

### Core Workflow

**Step 1: Show Concept and Identity**
- Define the show's core premise and unique angle
- Identify the target listener persona and their pain points
- Establish tone: conversational, investigative, educational, comedic
- Design the sonic identity: intro music, stingers, transitions, bed music
- Determine format: solo, co-hosted, interview, narrative, hybrid

**Step 2: Episode Planning and Structure**
- Develop a content calendar with episode themes and arcs
- Create a detailed episode rundown with segment timings
- Outline the narrative arc: hook, development, climax, resolution
- Prepare interview questions with follow-up branches
- Plan cold opens that immediately capture attention

**Step 3: Recording and Capture**
- Set up recording environment for optimal acoustics
- Conduct sound checks and level calibration
- Record with proper gain staging to avoid clipping
- Capture room tone for seamless editing
- Use markers or timestamps for key moments during recording

**Step 4: Editing and Post-Production**
- Remove unwanted noise, mouth clicks, breaths, and dead air
- Apply noise reduction and EQ to balance tonal quality
- Add compression to maintain consistent loudness throughout
- Layer in music beds, sound effects, and transitions
- Mix to target loudness standards (LUFS targets per platform)

**Step 5: Distribution and Promotion**
- Export in platform-optimized formats (MP3/AAC, bitrate, metadata)
- Write SEO-rich show notes with timestamps and links
- Create audiograms and social teasers for promotion
- Submit to directories: Apple Podcasts, Spotify, Google, Overcast
- Analyze download metrics and listener behavior for iteration

### Narrative Audio Framework

Construct each episode as a self-contained story with intentional pacing and emotional dynamics.

**The Audio Narrative Arc:**
Episodes follow a dramatic structure adapted for the ear. Without visual cues, the listener relies entirely on vocal tone, pacing, music, and sound design to stay engaged.

**Cold Open (0:00-0:45)**
Begin with the most compelling 30-45 seconds of the episode. This can be a provocative question, a dramatic clip pulled from later in the conversation, or an evocative scene-setting moment. The cold open is your hook — it must create tension or curiosity that demands resolution.

**Introduction (0:45-2:00)**
Deliver the show intro with consistent branding. State what the episode covers and why the listener should care. Frame the stakes clearly.

**Act One: Setup (2:00-10:00)**
Establish the context, introduce the guest or topic, and lay the groundwork. Build the world the listener will inhabit for the next 30-60 minutes. Use concrete details and specific anecdotes rather than abstract descriptions.

**Act Two: Development and Tension (10:00-30:00)**
This is the body of the episode. Introduce complications, contrasting viewpoints, surprising data, or escalating stakes. Vary the pacing — alternate between dense informational segments and breathing room. Use music beds to signal tonal shifts.

**Act Three: Resolution (30:00-40:00)**
Deliver the payoff. Synthesize insights, reveal conclusions, or let the guest arrive at their most powerful statement. The resolution should feel earned — a direct consequence of the journey taken.

**Outro and Call to Action (final 2-3 minutes)**
Recap key takeaways. Direct listeners to resources, next episodes, or community channels. End with a consistent sign-off that reinforces show identity.

**Pacing Techniques:**
- Use silence deliberately — a 2-second pause before a key point amplifies its impact
- Vary segment lengths to prevent rhythmic monotony
- Place the strongest content at the beginning and the end (primacy and recency)
- Insert "ear candy" — short sound design moments that reward attentive listening

### Sound Design and Mixing Framework

Shape the sonic landscape to create an immersive, professional listening experience.

**Tonal Balance and EQ:**
The human voice sits primarily between 85 Hz and 8 kHz. Apply a high-pass filter at 80 Hz to remove rumble. Boost presence in the 2-5 kHz range for clarity. Reduce muddiness in the 200-400 Hz range if the recording sounds boxy.

**Compression Strategy:**
Apply gentle compression (2:1 to 4:1 ratio) to even out vocal dynamics. Set the threshold so compression engages on louder passages without squashing natural expression. Use makeup gain to restore overall level. For multi-voice shows, compress each track independently before mixing.

**Loudness Standards:**
- Apple Podcasts: -16 LUFS (stereo), -19 LUFS (mono)
- Spotify: -14 LUFS integrated
- YouTube: -14 LUFS integrated
- General broadcast: -16 LUFS with -1 dB true peak maximum

**Music and Sound Design Layering:**
- Intro/outro music should be full and present, ducking under voice when overlapping
- Transition stingers should be 2-5 seconds, marking segment boundaries clearly
- Background music beds should sit 15-20 dB below voice level
- Sound effects should enhance narrative moments without distracting from speech

**Room Tone and Continuity:**
Always record 30 seconds of room tone at the start of each session. Use this to fill edits seamlessly. Crossfade all cuts (15-30 ms) to avoid audible clicks or jumps.

### Best Practices

- Record in the smallest, most acoustically treated room available — closets outperform studios without treatment
- Always record double-ender for remote interviews (each person records locally) for maximum audio quality
- Keep episodes to a consistent length — listeners build habits around predictable formats
- Front-load value: the first 3 minutes determine whether a new listener subscribes
- Edit ruthlessly — remove tangents, filler words, and anything that does not serve the narrative
- Use chapter markers for longer episodes to aid navigation and re-listening
- Batch record episodes to maintain momentum and reduce setup overhead
- Write show notes before editing — it forces you to identify the episode's core structure
- Normalize loudness across all episodes so listeners never need to adjust volume between releases
- Repurpose content: extract quotes for social, transcribe for blog posts, clip highlights for short-form video
- Test your podcast on multiple devices — earbuds, car speakers, laptop speakers — to catch mix issues
- Invest in a good microphone before anything else; a $100 dynamic mic beats a $500 condenser in an untreated room
- Always have a backup recording running

### Common Patterns

**Pattern: The Guest Interview Episode**
A technology podcast interviews a startup founder. The host prepares 12 questions organized into three thematic blocks: origin story, technical deep-dive, and future vision. The cold open uses a 20-second clip from the founder's most surprising answer. The host records a 90-second solo intro contextualizing why this guest matters now. During editing, the conversation is tightened from 58 minutes to 42 minutes by removing tangential stories and redundant explanations. Three music transitions mark the shift between thematic blocks. The outro includes a teaser for the next episode and a call to action for reviews.

**Pattern: The Solo Educational Episode**
A marketing consultant records a solo episode on "the hierarchy of content distribution." The episode opens with a listener question that frames the topic. The host walks through a five-level framework, spending 4-6 minutes on each level with specific examples. A light background music bed plays during the introduction and transitions but drops out during the teaching segments to maintain focus. The host records the episode in one take, then edits for pacing — removing pauses longer than 1.5 seconds and tightening transitions between levels.

**Pattern: The Narrative Documentary Episode**
A true-crime podcast constructs a 45-minute narrative episode. The producer assembles audio from three interviews, archival tape, and narration. The episode follows a chronological structure with a non-linear cold open that drops the listener into the climactic moment. Sound design includes ambient recreations of locations described by interviewees. The mix uses stereo panning to differentiate between narration (center) and interview clips (slightly off-center). Music builds in intensity across the episode, reaching full orchestration only in the final five minutes.

### Output Formats

**Episode Plan Document:**
A comprehensive production document containing the episode concept, narrative arc, segment breakdown with target timestamps, guest research notes, question list, and post-production instructions. Delivered as a structured markdown document ready for the production team.

**Show Notes and Metadata Package:**
A distribution-ready package containing the episode title, description (both short and long form), timestamped show notes, guest bio, resource links, SEO keywords, and social media copy for promotion across platforms.

**Audio Production Spec Sheet:**
A technical reference document specifying recording format, sample rate, bit depth, target loudness, EQ settings, compression parameters, export formats per platform, and file naming conventions for the show's entire catalog.
