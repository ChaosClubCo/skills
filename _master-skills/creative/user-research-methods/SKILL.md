# User Research Methods Skill

## Overview
Comprehensive guide to qualitative and quantitative user research methods for product discovery, validation, and continuous improvement. Covers 2025 best practices for remote research, synthesis frameworks, and ethical considerations.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Planning discovery research for new products/features
- Validating design concepts before development
- Understanding user behavior and pain points
- Measuring product usability and satisfaction
- Prioritizing roadmap based on user needs

---

## Research Framework (Jobs-to-be-Done)

### Core Philosophy
**"People don't want a quarter-inch drill, they want a quarter-inch hole."**

Focus on:
1. **Jobs:** What is the user trying to accomplish?
2. **Pains:** What frustrates them about current solutions?
3. **Gains:** What would make their experience better?

**Claude action:** When framing research questions, start with jobs-to-be-done perspective.

---

## Research Types by Phase

### Discovery (What to Build)
**Goal:** Identify problems worth solving

**Methods:**
- User interviews (exploratory)
- Contextual inquiry (observational)
- Diary studies (longitudinal)
- Surveys (broad feedback)

**Output:** Problem statements, opportunity areas

---

### Validation (Does This Solve the Problem?)
**Goal:** Test concepts before investing in development

**Methods:**
- Usability testing (prototype evaluation)
- A/B testing (quantitative)
- Card sorting (information architecture)
- Tree testing (navigation validation)

**Output:** Go/no-go decision, design refinements

---

### Optimization (How to Improve)
**Goal:** Iterate on existing features

**Methods:**
- Analytics review (behavioral data)
- Heatmaps / session recordings
- Feedback surveys (NPS, CSAT)
- Usability testing (existing product)

**Output:** Prioritized improvements, UX debt list

## Qualitative Methods

### 1. User Interviews

#### When to Use:
- Early discovery (understanding context)
- Exploring motivations and attitudes
- Gathering stories and anecdotes

#### Structure (60 minutes):
1. **Intro (5 min):** Build rapport, explain purpose
2. **Background (10 min):** Demographics, role, experience
3. **Context (20 min):** Current tools, workflows, pain points
4. **Exploration (20 min):** Deep dive on specific topics
5. **Wrap-up (5 min):** Any questions? Compensation details

#### Question Types:

**✅ Open-ended (Good):**
- "Tell me about the last time you..."
- "Walk me through your process for..."
- "What frustrates you most about..."
- "How do you currently solve..."

**❌ Leading/Closed (Bad):**
- "Do you like this feature?" (Yes/no)
- "Wouldn't it be great if..." (Leading)
- "Why don't you use [competitor]?" (Assumes non-use)

**Follow-ups (5 Whys):**
- "Tell me more about that..."
- "Why is that important?"
- "Can you give me an example?"
- "What do you mean by [term]?"

---

#### Sample Interview Guide (SaaS Analytics Tool)

**Research question:** How do marketing managers track campaign performance?

**Intro:**
"Thanks for joining! I'm researching how marketing teams measure campaign success. There are no right or wrong answers—I'm just trying to understand your workflow. This will take about an hour. Sound good?"

**Background:**
1. Tell me about your role and responsibilities.
2. What tools do you use day-to-day?
3. How long have you been doing this type of work?

**Context:**
4. Walk me through a recent campaign you ran.
5. How did you measure its success?
6. What data sources did you pull from?

**Pain points:**
7. What's the most frustrating part of tracking campaign performance?
8. Tell me about a time when you couldn't find the data you needed.
9. How much time do you spend pulling reports?

**Current solutions:**
10. What tools or processes do you use to solve this?
11. What do you like about [current tool]?
12. What would you change if you could?

**Future state:**
13. If you could wave a magic wand, how would this work?
14. What would make your job easier?

**Wrap-up:**
15. Is there anything I didn't ask about that's important?

#### Recruitment (Finding Participants)

**Internal:**
- Customer support tickets (people with issues)
- Sales pipeline (prospects considering purchase)
- Power users (highest usage)

**External:**
- **User Interviews** (platform for recruiting)
- **Respondent.io** (B2B professionals)
- **Ethnio** (website intercept)
- LinkedIn outreach (cold, but works for B2B)

**Incentives (2025 standards):**
- **General consumers:** $50-75 for 60 min
- **Professionals (B2B):** $100-200 for 60 min
- **Executives/specialists:** $200-500 for 60 min
- **Existing customers:** Gift cards, product credits

**Screening criteria:**
- Job title / role
- Company size
- Tools currently used
- Frequency of task
- Exclude: Competitors, UX professionals (overly analytical)

### 2. Usability Testing

#### When to Use:
- Evaluating designs (prototypes or live product)
- Identifying friction points
- Measuring task success rates

#### Structure (45-60 minutes):
1. **Intro (5 min):** Explain think-aloud protocol
2. **Tasks (30-40 min):** 3-5 realistic scenarios
3. **Debrief (10 min):** Overall impressions, suggestions

#### Think-Aloud Protocol:
"As you complete each task, please narrate your thoughts out loud. Tell me what you're looking for, what you expect to happen, what confuses you, etc. Pretend I'm not here—just talk through your process."

**Moderator role:**
- Stay silent during tasks (don't help!)
- Observe clicks, hesitations, errors
- Ask "What are you thinking?" if stuck
- Probe after task: "What did you expect there?"

---

#### Sample Usability Test Plan (E-commerce Checkout)

**Research question:** Can users complete checkout without errors?

**Tasks:**
1. **Add to cart:** "You want to buy a blue t-shirt in size medium. Go ahead."
2. **Apply coupon:** "You have a 10% off code: SAVE10. Use it."
3. **Checkout:** "Complete the purchase using this test credit card."

**Success metrics:**
- Task completion rate (% who finish)
- Time on task (average seconds)
- Error rate (wrong clicks, backtracking)
- Satisfaction (post-task rating 1-5)

**Sample size:**
- **5 participants:** Finds ~85% of usability issues (Jakob Nielsen)
- **8-10 participants:** More robust, better for segmentation (e.g., mobile vs desktop)

---

#### Remote vs In-Person

**Remote (Zoom, Teams):**
- ✅ Cheaper, faster recruitment
- ✅ Natural environment (participant's home)
- ❌ Harder to read body language
- ❌ Technical issues (screen sharing, internet)

**In-Person:**
- ✅ Better rapport, body language
- ✅ Mobile device testing (easier)
- ❌ Expensive (travel, lab rental)
- ❌ Limited to local participants

**2025 trend:** Hybrid—remote by default, in-person for sensitive/complex research (medical devices, financial products).

### 3. Contextual Inquiry (Field Research)

#### When to Use:
- Understanding workflow in natural environment
- Observing actual behavior (not self-reported)
- Discovering unstated needs

**Structure:**
1. **Shadow the user** (don't interrupt)
2. **Ask clarifying questions** (after observation)
3. **Document artifacts** (tools, workarounds, post-its)

**Example:** Researching how nurses use EMR (electronic medical records)
- Visit hospital
- Shadow nurse during shift
- Observe: How often do they check system? What do they write on paper? Where do they get frustrated?
- Discover: Nurses keep a paper notepad because EMR is too slow to open during rounds (insight!)

**Time required:** 2-4 hours per session (exhausting but rich data)

---

### 4. Diary Studies

#### When to Use:
- Longitudinal behavior (days, weeks)
- Infrequent tasks (happens once a week, hard to observe in one session)
- Emotional journey over time

**Structure:**
1. Participant logs experiences daily/weekly
2. Researcher reviews entries, asks follow-ups
3. Final interview to synthesize patterns

**Tools:**
- **dscout** (mobile diary study platform)
- **Ethnio** (web-based)
- Google Forms + calendar reminders (DIY)

**Example:** Fitness app usage
- Users log workouts for 2 weeks
- Capture: When, where, what motivated them, what frustrated them
- Discover: Most users work out at 6am before work, hate opening app (too slow), want faster logging

## Quantitative Methods

### 1. Surveys

#### When to Use:
- Large sample size (100s or 1000s)
- Measuring satisfaction, preferences
- Prioritizing features (votes)

#### Question Types:

**Rating scales:**
- Likert (1-5): "How satisfied are you with..." (Strongly Disagree → Strongly Agree)
- NPS (0-10): "How likely are you to recommend..."
- Semantic differential: "Confusing ↔ Intuitive"

**Multiple choice:**
- Single select: "What's your primary role?"
- Multi-select: "Which features do you use? (Select all)"

**Open-ended:**
- "What's the most frustrating part of..."
- Use sparingly (hard to analyze at scale)

---

#### Sample Survey (Feature Prioritization)

**Goal:** Prioritize roadmap for Q1 2025

**Questions:**
1. **Screener:** Do you currently use [Product]? (Yes/No)
2. **Usage:** How often do you use [Product]? (Daily, Weekly, Monthly, Rarely)
3. **Feature importance:** Rate how important each feature is to you (1-5)
   - Dark mode
   - Keyboard shortcuts
   - Export to CSV
   - Mobile app
4. **Feature request:** What feature would you most like to see added? (Open-ended)
5. **NPS:** How likely are you to recommend [Product] to a colleague? (0-10)
6. **Follow-up:** Can we contact you for a 30-minute interview? (Yes/No + Email)

**Distribution:**
- Email to user base (best for existing customers)
- In-app prompt (pop-up after specific action)
- Social media (broad but lower quality)

**Sample size:**
- **100 responses:** Directional insights
- **400+ responses:** Statistical significance for A/B tests

### 2. A/B Testing

#### When to Use:
- Testing two variations (design, copy, flow)
- Measuring impact on metrics (clicks, conversions, retention)

**Structure:**
1. **Hypothesis:** "Changing CTA from 'Learn More' to 'Get Started' will increase signups by 10%"
2. **Split traffic:** 50% see A (control), 50% see B (variant)
3. **Measure:** Track conversion rate for both groups
4. **Analyze:** Statistical significance (p < 0.05)

**Metrics:**
- **Primary:** The main goal (signup rate, purchase rate)
- **Secondary:** Other impacts (time on page, bounce rate)
- **Guardrail:** Metrics that shouldn't degrade (page load time, error rate)

**Tools:**
- **Optimizely** (enterprise)
- **VWO** (mid-market)
- **Google Optimize** (free, sunset 2023 → use GA4 experiments)
- **LaunchDarkly** (feature flags)

**Sample size calculator:** [Evan Miller's AB Test Calculator](https://www.evanmiller.org/ab-testing/sample-size.html)

---

### 3. Analytics Review

#### When to Use:
- Understanding actual user behavior (not self-reported)
- Identifying drop-off points
- Measuring feature adoption

**Tools:**
- **Google Analytics / GA4** (web traffic)
- **Mixpanel / Amplitude** (product analytics)
- **Hotjar / FullStory** (session recordings, heatmaps)

**Key metrics:**
- **Acquisition:** Where do users come from?
- **Activation:** Do they complete onboarding?
- **Engagement:** How often do they return?
- **Retention:** Do they come back after 7 days? 30 days?
- **Revenue:** Do they convert to paid?

**AARRR Pirate Metrics:**
- Acquisition → Activation → Retention → Referral → Revenue

**Example insight:**
- 60% of users drop off during signup (pain point: too many fields)
- Users who complete onboarding tutorial have 3x higher retention (opportunity: improve tutorial visibility)

---

### 4. Card Sorting

#### When to Use:
- Organizing information architecture
- Validating navigation structure
- Understanding mental models

**Types:**
1. **Open card sort:** Users create their own categories
2. **Closed card sort:** Users sort cards into predefined categories

**Process:**
1. List all content items (products, features, pages) on "cards"
2. Ask users to group cards that belong together
3. (Open sort) Ask users to name each group
4. Analyze: Which items are consistently grouped? What patterns emerge?

**Tools:**
- **Optimal Workshop** (online card sorting)
- **UserZoom** (includes tree testing)
- Figma / Miro (DIY with digital sticky notes)

**Example:** E-commerce site navigation
- Cards: "Shirts", "Pants", "Shoes", "Hats", "Belts", "Socks"
- Users might group: "Tops" (Shirts, Hats), "Bottoms" (Pants, Shoes), "Accessories" (Belts, Socks)
- Result: Information architecture based on user mental models

## Synthesis & Analysis

### Affinity Mapping (Qualitative)

**Purpose:** Organize interview/test findings into themes

**Process:**
1. **Transcribe:** Convert audio to text (Otter.ai, Trint)
2. **Extract quotes:** Pull verbatim user statements
3. **Write observations on sticky notes:** One per note
4. **Group similar notes:** Find patterns
5. **Label themes:** "Pain points", "Workarounds", "Feature requests"
6. **Prioritize:** Which themes appear most often?

**Example:**
```
[Pain Points Theme]
  ├── "It takes forever to load" (3 participants)
  ├── "I don't know where to find settings" (5 participants)
  └── "The mobile app crashes daily" (2 participants)
```

**Insight:** Navigation is the biggest issue (mentioned by 5/8 participants).

### Jobs-to-be-Done Canvas

**Format:**
```
Job: [User goal]
  When: [Situation]
  I want to: [Action]
  So I can: [Outcome]

Current Solution:
  [How they do it today]

Pain Points:
  [What frustrates them]

Desired Gains:
  [What would make it better]
```

**Example (Email client):**
```
Job: Manage inbox overload
  When: I return from vacation
  I want to: Quickly triage 500 emails
  So I can: Get back to important work

Current Solution:
  - Manually archive or delete emails
  - Use search to find important threads

Pain Points:
  - Takes 2 hours to clear inbox
  - Miss important emails in the noise
  - No bulk actions

Desired Gains:
  - Auto-categorize (priority, newsletters, receipts)
  - Bulk archive by sender
  - Smart reminders for follow-ups
```

---

### Quantitative Analysis (Survey/A/B Test)

**Statistical Significance:**
- **P-value < 0.05:** Result is statistically significant (not due to chance)
- **Confidence interval:** Range where true value likely falls (e.g., conversion rate is 5% ± 1%)

**Tools:**
- **Excel/Google Sheets:** Basic stats (AVERAGE, STDEV, T.TEST)
- **R / Python:** Advanced analysis (regression, clustering)
- **Amplitude / Mixpanel:** Built-in statistical tests

**Example A/B test result:**
```
Control (A):  1000 visitors, 50 conversions (5.0%)
Variant (B):  1000 visitors, 70 conversions (7.0%)
Difference:   +2.0 percentage points
P-value:      0.03 (< 0.05, statistically significant!)
```

**Decision:** Ship variant B (40% relative lift in conversions).

## Deliverables

### 1. Research Report

**Structure:**
- **Executive summary:** Key findings (1 page)
- **Research goals:** What questions were we answering?
- **Methodology:** Who, when, how
- **Findings:** Themes with evidence (quotes, screenshots)
- **Recommendations:** Prioritized action items
- **Appendix:** Full data (transcripts, charts)

**Audience:** Product managers, designers, engineers, leadership

**Format:** Notion, Google Docs, Slides (for presentations)

---

### 2. User Personas

**Purpose:** Shared understanding of target users

**Format:**
```
Name: "Marketing Manager Mary"
Role: Marketing Manager at B2B SaaS (50-200 employees)
Goals:
  - Prove ROI of marketing spend
  - Automate reporting
Pain Points:
  - Data scattered across tools
  - Manual reporting takes 5 hours/week
Behaviors:
  - Uses Google Analytics, HubSpot, Salesforce
  - Pulls reports every Monday morning
Quote: "I spend more time making slides than analyzing data."
```

**Anti-pattern:** Fictional demographics (age, hobbies) that don't affect design decisions. Focus on behaviors, goals, pain points.

---

### 3. Journey Map

**Purpose:** Visualize user experience across touchpoints

**Axes:**
- **X-axis:** Stages (Awareness → Consideration → Purchase → Use → Support)
- **Y-axis:** Experience (positive, neutral, negative)

**Layers:**
- Actions (what user does)
- Thoughts (what they're thinking)
- Emotions (how they feel)
- Pain points (where they struggle)
- Opportunities (how to improve)

**Tool:** Miro, FigJam, or custom in Figma

## Ethical Considerations

### Informed Consent

**Required disclosures:**
1. **Purpose:** What you're researching and why
2. **Recording:** If audio/video/screen recording
3. **Data usage:** How you'll use their data (anonymized, internal only, etc.)
4. **Withdrawal:** They can stop anytime, no penalty
5. **Compensation:** How and when they'll be paid

**Template:**
"This research is to improve [Product]. We'll record this session for analysis. Your data will be anonymized and only shared with our team. You can stop at any time and still receive your $75 gift card. By continuing, you consent to participate."

---

### Privacy (GDPR, CCPA)

**Requirements:**
- **Data minimization:** Only collect what's needed
- **Anonymization:** Remove personally identifiable info (PII) from transcripts
- **Retention limits:** Delete data after project ends (or set timeline)
- **Right to deletion:** If participant requests, delete their data

**Tools:**
- **Grain, Otter.ai:** Auto-redact PII (names, emails)
- **Dovetail, Aurelius:** Research repositories with access controls

---

### Bias Awareness

**Types of bias:**
1. **Selection bias:** Recruiting only power users (miss struggling users)
2. **Confirmation bias:** Only hearing what you want to hear
3. **Leading questions:** "Don't you think this is easier?"
4. **Moderator influence:** Nodding/smiling when user does "right" thing

**Mitigation:**
- Recruit diverse participants (roles, experience levels, demographics)
- Use a discussion guide (stay consistent across sessions)
- Have a second observer take notes (catch what you miss)
- Review transcripts, not just memory

## Common Pitfalls

### ❌ Anti-Patterns:
1. **Asking what users want:** Users don't know (ask about pain points instead)
2. **Testing with stakeholders/team:** They know too much (biased)
3. **Testing with 1-2 users:** Not enough for patterns (need 5+ for usability)
4. **Asking yes/no questions:** No depth (ask open-ended)
5. **Helping during usability tests:** Defeats the purpose (observe silently)
6. **Ignoring negative feedback:** Confirmation bias (listen to criticism)
7. **Skipping synthesis:** Data dump without insights (spend time analyzing)

### ✅ Best Practices:
1. **Recruit the right users** (target persona, not anyone available)
2. **Test early and often** (every sprint if possible)
3. **Combine methods** (interviews + testing + analytics)
4. **Triangulate findings** (look for patterns across participants)
5. **Share insights widely** (video clips, quotes, journey maps)
6. **Turn insights into action** (prioritized roadmap, design changes)

## Claude Workflow Integration

### When User Requests Research Planning:

1. **Clarify goal:**
   - What decisions are you trying to make?
   - What do you already know?
   - What's the timeline?

2. **Recommend methods:**
   - Qualitative for "why" (interviews, usability tests)
   - Quantitative for "how many" (surveys, analytics, A/B tests)

3. **Generate research plan:**
   - Research questions
   - Method selection
   - Recruitment criteria
   - Discussion guide / test plan
   - Timeline and budget

4. **Provide templates:**
   - Interview script
   - Usability test tasks
   - Survey questions
   - Consent form

## Gaps & Blindspots

### Known Limitations:
- **Participant honesty:** Self-reported behavior ≠ actual behavior (use analytics to validate)
- **Lab effect:** Behavior changes when observed (Hawthorne effect)
- **Sample bias:** Participants who volunteer are different from general population
- **Remote research:** Harder to build rapport, read body language
- **Translation:** International research may lose nuance in translation

### Unknown Unknowns:
- **AI-assisted synthesis:** Tools like Dovetail AI, Notably AI (quality concerns, bias amplification)
- **Synthetic users:** AI-generated personas for testing (ethical implications)
- **Passive research:** Always-on session recording (privacy backlash)
- **Voice/emotion AI:** Detecting frustration via tone (accuracy, ethics)
