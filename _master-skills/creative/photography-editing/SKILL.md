# Photography Editing

> Shape light, color, and detail into images that communicate with precision and emotion.

## Description

This skill encompasses the complete post-production photography workflow, from raw file processing and color correction through retouching, compositing, and export optimization. It provides frameworks for non-destructive editing, color grading for mood and narrative, skin retouching that preserves natural texture, and batch processing for consistent output across large shoots. The skill addresses both the technical mechanics of editing software and the aesthetic judgment required to make images resonate with their intended audience and purpose.

## Instructions

### Core Workflow

**Step 1: Import and Organization**
- Ingest files with a consistent naming convention: Date_Project_Sequence
- Create a folder hierarchy: Project > Selects > Edits > Finals > Exports
- Perform an initial cull: reject obvious failures, flag strong candidates, rate selects
- Apply metadata: keywords, copyright, caption information
- Back up original files before any editing begins — never edit originals

**Step 2: Global Adjustments (Raw Processing)**
- Set white balance for accurate or intentionally shifted color temperature
- Adjust exposure to place the histogram correctly — protect highlights, open shadows
- Set black and white points to establish the full tonal range
- Apply lens corrections: distortion, chromatic aberration, vignetting
- Reduce noise and apply initial sharpening appropriate to ISO and intended output

**Step 3: Tonal and Color Refinement**
- Shape the tonal curve to establish contrast character: linear, S-curve, matte, high-key
- Adjust individual color channels using HSL controls for targeted color shifts
- Apply split toning or color grading to shadows, midtones, and highlights independently
- Use local adjustments (gradients, radial filters, brushes) for targeted exposure and color control
- Check color accuracy against known references if commercial or product work

**Step 4: Retouching and Detail Work**
- Remove distractions: sensor dust, stray objects, unwanted elements
- Perform skin retouching using frequency separation or dodge-and-burn techniques
- Maintain natural texture — over-smoothing destroys authenticity and trust
- Sharpen for detail emphasis using luminosity masking to avoid sharpening noise
- Apply local dodging and burning to sculpt light and direct the viewer's eye

**Step 5: Export and Delivery**
- Export at resolution and format appropriate to the deliverable: web, print, social
- Apply output sharpening calibrated to the medium: screen, matte print, glossy print
- Convert color profile: sRGB for web, Adobe RGB or ProPhoto RGB for print
- Add watermarks or copyright overlays if required
- Verify file size, dimensions, and quality meet delivery specifications

### Color Correction and Grading Framework

Separate the technical discipline of correction from the creative art of grading — they serve different purposes and follow different rules.

**Color Correction: Getting It Right**
Color correction is the process of making the image accurately represent the scene as the human eye perceived it, or as the creative intent demands as a neutral starting point.

**White Balance Calibration:**
Begin by neutralizing the color cast. If a gray card or color checker was shot on location, sample it to set precise white balance. Without a reference, use the eyedropper tool on an element you know to be neutral — white paper, gray concrete, a white shirt. Adjust temperature (blue-yellow axis) and tint (green-magenta axis) until neutrals appear truly neutral.

**Exposure Correction:**
Use the histogram as your guide. A well-exposed image uses the full tonal range without clipping. Recover blown highlights by reducing the highlights slider. Open crushed shadows by lifting the shadows slider. Set the overall brightness with the exposure control so the subject falls in the desired tonal zone.

**Color Balance Per Channel:**
If the overall correction leaves individual colors skewed, use the HSL panel or curves per channel. Skin tones are the most critical reference — human perception is extraordinarily sensitive to skin color inaccuracies. Healthy skin has a hue between 10 and 40 degrees on the vectorscope regardless of ethnicity; saturation and luminance vary.

**Color Grading: Setting the Mood**
Color grading is the intentional departure from neutral to evoke emotion, establish period, or create visual identity.

**Tonal Mood Mapping:**
- **Warm and nostalgic:** Push shadows toward amber/brown, highlights toward gold. Desaturate blues slightly. Lift the black point for a matte, film-like quality.
- **Cool and clinical:** Push shadows toward blue-teal, keep highlights neutral or slightly cool. Increase clarity. Maintain deep blacks for contrast.
- **Cinematic and dramatic:** Use complementary color contrast — teal in shadows, orange in highlights. Crush blacks slightly. Apply a subtle S-curve for punchy contrast.
- **Bright and editorial:** Keep overall exposure high. Desaturate slightly. Push toward pastel tones. Minimal contrast in shadows, open midtones.
- **Dark and moody:** Underexpose globally by 0.5-1.0 stops. Deepen shadows. Use a single accent color against desaturated backgrounds. Vignette subtly.

**Consistency Across a Series:**
When grading a multi-image series, edit one hero image to perfection, then sync or copy settings across the set. Adjust individual images only for exposure and white balance differences. The grade itself — curve shape, split tone values, HSL adjustments — must remain identical for visual cohesion.

### Retouching Philosophy and Technique

Approach retouching as sculpture rather than painting — remove what distracts, enhance what exists, and never fabricate what was not there.

**The Retouching Hierarchy:**
Address issues in order of impact, from global to local.

**Level 1: Clean-Up**
Remove temporary distractions that were not part of the intended scene. Sensor dust, stray hairs, lint on clothing, background clutter. Use the clone stamp and healing brush with careful source selection. Match texture, lighting direction, and color of the surrounding area.

**Level 2: Skin and Surface**
For portrait work, address blemishes while preserving skin texture. The frequency separation technique splits the image into texture (high frequency) and color/tone (low frequency), allowing you to smooth tonal transitions without destroying pores and fine detail. Alternatively, use dodge-and-burn at low opacity (3-8%) to manually even out tonal variations while retaining complete texture.

**The Cardinal Rule of Skin Retouching:**
Zoom to 100% and evaluate. If the skin looks like plastic, wax, or an oil painting, you have gone too far. Pull back until natural texture is clearly visible. Authentic texture communicates health and honesty. Over-retouching communicates artifice and erodes viewer trust.

**Level 3: Sculpting with Light**
Use dodge-and-burn to shape the three-dimensionality of the subject. Brighten areas that catch natural light, deepen areas that fall into shadow. This technique follows the same principles as chiaroscuro in painting — building form through the careful modulation of light and dark.

- Dodge highlights along the bridge of the nose, cheekbones, forehead, and chin
- Burn shadows under the jawline, beside the nose, and in the eye sockets
- Work on a separate gray layer set to Overlay or Soft Light blend mode for non-destructive control

**Level 4: Eyes and Details**
Eyes are the focal point of any portrait. Brighten the iris slightly (never the whites — this looks unnatural). Enhance the catchlight. Sharpen the eyelashes and iris detail subtly. Remove visible red veins only if they are distractingly prominent.

### Best Practices

- Always edit non-destructively: use adjustment layers, smart objects, virtual copies, and sidecar files
- Calibrate your monitor monthly — an uncalibrated display renders all color decisions unreliable
- Edit in a controlled lighting environment: consistent, neutral-colored ambient light at moderate brightness
- Step away from a complex edit and return with fresh eyes before finalizing — adaptation bias is real
- Develop and save presets for recurring looks, but always fine-tune per image after applying
- Use before/after comparisons frequently during editing to maintain perspective on cumulative changes
- Resist the temptation to over-process — the best edits are invisible to the viewer
- Maintain a consistent editing style within a project, series, or brand to build visual identity
- Learn keyboard shortcuts for your primary editing tool — speed enables creative flow
- Export a proof at final delivery size and view it on multiple devices before sending to the client
- Keep your raw files forever — they are your negatives, and future tools will extract more from them
- When in doubt, less is more: subtle adjustments that enhance the photograph's existing qualities always outperform heavy-handed transformations
- Color-check critical images on both a calibrated monitor and a mobile device — most viewers will see your work on phones
- Document your editing decisions in notes or metadata for future reference and consistency

### Common Patterns

**Pattern: The Wedding Gallery Edit**
A photographer delivers 400 images from a wedding. The editing workflow begins with a full cull, reducing to 350 selects. A hero image from the ceremony is edited to establish the grade: warm, slightly desaturated, lifted blacks for a romantic film quality. The preset is synced across all ceremony images, then individually adjusted for exposure and white balance variations between indoor and outdoor lighting. Reception images receive a separate but complementary grade with warmer tones and slightly more contrast to match the golden-hour and artificial lighting. Portraits receive Level 2 retouching: blemish removal, under-eye lightening, and subtle dodge-and-burn. The full gallery is exported as high-resolution JPEGs in sRGB for the online gallery and Adobe RGB TIFFs for the client's album printer.

**Pattern: The Product Photography Set**
An e-commerce brand shoots 50 products on white seamless. Each image requires precise color correction to ensure product colors match physical samples. The editor uses a color checker photographed at the beginning of the session to build a camera-specific profile. White balance is set exactly to 5500K. Background is cleaned to pure white (RGB 255,255,255) using curves and masking. Each product receives clone stamp cleanup for dust and imperfections. Shadow detail is carefully preserved to maintain the three-dimensional quality of flat-lit products. All images are exported at exactly 2000x2000 pixels, sRGB, JPEG quality 90, with consistent padding and centering per the brand's style guide.

**Pattern: The Editorial Portrait Series**
A magazine commissions a portrait series of five subjects. The photographer and editor agree on a cinematic, teal-and-orange grade with dramatic contrast. Each portrait is individually retouched using frequency separation for skin, dodge-and-burn for facial sculpting, and careful eye enhancement. The grade is built on the first portrait and adapted to each subject's skin tone — the teal-orange balance shifts subtly to complement each individual. Background elements are slightly desaturated to ensure the subject dominates the frame. Final files are delivered as 16-bit TIFFs in Adobe RGB at 300 DPI, with a separate set of sRGB web exports for the publication's online edition.

### Output Formats

**Editing Direction Brief:**
A comprehensive document specifying the visual direction for a photo editing project, including mood references, color temperature targets, contrast style, retouching scope, and delivery specifications. Serves as the contract between photographer and editor.

**Preset and Style Guide:**
A detailed specification of all adjustment parameters for a particular photographic look, including tone curve values, HSL adjustments, color grading settings, and effects. Enables consistent application across teams, shoots, and time periods.

**Export and Delivery Specification:**
A technical document defining exact output requirements per deliverable type: file format, color profile, resolution, dimensions, sharpening settings, naming conventions, and delivery method. Ensures every exported file meets its intended use case without rework.
