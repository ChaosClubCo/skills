# Research Methods

## Overview

Research methods provide the systematic approaches for gathering, analyzing, and interpreting information to answer business and product questions. Rigorous methodology separates evidence-based decision-making from opinion-based guessing, enabling organizations to invest confidently in directions supported by data.

Effective research combines qualitative methods (understanding why through observation and conversation) with quantitative methods (measuring what and how much through data and surveys). The choice of method depends on the question, available resources, and required confidence level.

Great researchers know not just how to conduct studies but when to use which method, how to design unbiased instruments, and how to translate findings into actionable recommendations that stakeholders will embrace.

### Why This Matters

Decisions made without research often fail in market. Research reduces risk by testing assumptions before major investments, surfacing customer needs before building products, and quantifying opportunities before committing resources. The cost of research is typically a fraction of the cost of building the wrong thing.

## When to Use

### Primary Triggers

- Exploring new market opportunities
- Understanding customer needs and behaviors
- Validating product concepts
- Measuring satisfaction and experience
- Investigating usability issues
- Sizing market potential
- Testing messaging and positioning

### Specific Use Cases

| Research Question | Recommended Method | Output |
|------------------|-------------------|--------|
| What problems do users have? | Interviews, Observation | Need statements |
| Which concept do users prefer? | Concept testing | Preference data |
| Can users complete tasks? | Usability testing | Task success metrics |
| How satisfied are customers? | Survey | Satisfaction scores |
| How large is the market? | Secondary + Survey | Market size estimate |
| Why are users churning? | Exit interviews + Data | Churn drivers |

#### Method Selection

```markdown
## Method Selection Guide

### Decision Matrix

| Question Type | Best Methods | Sample Size | Timeline |
|--------------|--------------|-------------|----------|
| Exploratory (why, how) | Interviews, Observation | 5-15 | 2-4 weeks |
| Descriptive (what, who) | Survey, Analytics | 100-1000+ | 2-6 weeks |
| Evaluative (how well) | Usability test, A/B test | 5-20 or stat sig | 1-4 weeks |
| Causal (does X cause Y) | Experiment | Statistical sample | 4-8 weeks |

### Method Comparison

| Method | Strengths | Limitations |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| Interviews | Rich context, flexibility | Time-intensive, not generalizable |
| Surveys | Scalable, quantifiable | Surface-level, response bias |
| Observation | Actual behavior | Observer effect, interpretation |
| Usability tests | Direct feedback | Artificial context |
| A/B tests | Causal inference | Limited scope, requires traffic |
| Analytics | Real behavior, scale | No "why", correlation only |
| Secondary research | Fast, comprehensive | May be dated, not specific |

### Choosing Qual vs. Quant

Use Qualitative When:
- Exploring new territory
- Understanding context and motivation
- Generating hypotheses
- Depth over breadth needed

Use Quantitative When:
- Validating hypotheses
- Measuring frequency or magnitude
- Comparing options statistically
- Generalizing to population
```

### Phase 2: Qualitative Methods

#### Interview Methodology

```markdown
## Interview Best Practices

### Types of Interviews
| Type | Duration | Depth | Best For |
|------|----------|-------|----------|
| Screening | 10-15 min | Surface | Participant qualification |
| Exploratory | 45-60 min | Deep | Open-ended discovery |
| Contextual | 60-90 min | Very deep | In-environment observation |
| Expert | 30-45 min | Focused | Domain knowledge |

### Interview Structure

#### Opening (5 min)
- Introduction and rapport building
- Explain purpose (learning, not selling)
- Confirm recording consent
- Set expectations for time

#### Background (10 min)
- Role and context questions
- Relevant history and experience
- Establish baseline understanding

#### Core Exploration (25-35 min)
- Open-ended questions about topic
- Follow-up probes for depth
- Concrete examples and stories
- Observe non-verbal cues

#### Summary and Close (5 min)
- Summarize key points heard
- Ask for anything missed
- Thank and explain next steps

### Probing Techniques
| Technique | Example | Use When |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| Elaboration | "Tell me more about that" | Surface-level answer |
| Clarification | "What do you mean by [X]?" | Ambiguous statement |
| Contrast | "How is that different from [Y]?" | Understanding distinctions |
| Example | "Can you give me an example?" | Abstract concept |
| Emotion | "How did that make you feel?" | Understanding motivation |
| Silence | [Pause and wait] | Giving space to think |
```

#### Observation Methods

```markdown
## Observation Research Guide

### Observation Types
| Type | Description | Best For |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Naturalistic | Observe in natural setting | True behavior |
| Participant | Researcher joins activity | Deep understanding |
| Structured | Checklist-guided observation | Specific behaviors |
| Shadowing | Follow user through tasks | Full context |

### Field Observation Protocol

#### Before
- Define observation objectives
- Create observation guide
- Gain access to environment
- Prepare documentation tools

#### During
- Note what people say (quotes)
- Note what people do (actions)
- Note environment details
- Note artifacts and tools used
- Note emotions and reactions
- Avoid interpretation in notes

#### After
- Debrief immediately
- Organize raw notes
- Identify patterns
- Generate insights

### Observation Note Format
| Time | What I Saw | What I Heard | My Interpretation |
|------|------------|--------------|-------------------|
| [Time] | [Observable behavior] | [Verbatim quote] | [My meaning-making] |
```

### Phase 3: Quantitative Methods

#### Survey Design

```markdown
## Survey Design Framework

### Survey Planning
| Element | Consideration |
|---------|--------------|
| Purpose | What decisions will this inform? |
| Population | Who are we surveying? |
| Sample | How many responses needed? |
| Mode | Online, phone, in-person? |
| Length | Target completion time (5-10 min ideal) |
| Incentive | Payment or reward? |

### Question Types
| Type | Use For | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Multiple choice | Category selection | "Which best describes your role?" |
| Rating scale | Attitudes, satisfaction | "How satisfied are you? (1-5)" |
| Ranking | Prioritization | "Rank these features by importance" |
| Matrix | Multiple items, same scale | Satisfaction with various attributes |
| Open text | Explanation, detail | "What would you improve?" |
| NPS | Loyalty measure | "How likely to recommend? (0-10)" |

### Question Writing Best Practices
- Use simple, clear language
- Ask one thing at a time (no double-barreled)
- Avoid leading questions
- Balance positive/negative framing
- Provide exhaustive, mutually exclusive options
- Include "Don't know" / "N/A" when appropriate
- Consider order effects

### Survey Flow
1. Screening questions (if needed)
2. Warm-up questions (easy, engaging)
3. Core questions (most important)
4. Sensitive questions (demographics, income)
5. Open-ended questions (optional elaboration)
6. Thank you and close

### Sample Size Calculator
| Margin of Error | Confidence 95% | Sample Needed |
|-----------------|----------------|---------------|
| +/- 10% | 95% | 97 |
| +/- 5% | 95% | 385 |
| +/- 3% | 95% | 1,067 |
| +/- 2% | 95% | 2,401 |
```

#### Statistical Analysis Basics

```markdown
## Analysis Fundamentals

### Descriptive Statistics
| Measure | Use | Calculation |
|---------|-----|-------------|
| Mean | Average value | Sum / Count |
| Median | Middle value | 50th percentile |
| Mode | Most common | Highest frequency |
| Std Dev | Spread | Distance from mean |
| Range | Span | Max - Min |

### Inferential Statistics
| Test | Use Case | Requirements |
|------|----------|--------------|
| t-test | Compare two groups | Continuous, normal |
| Chi-square | Compare categories | Categorical |
| ANOVA | Compare 3+ groups | Continuous, normal |
| Correlation | Relationship strength | Two continuous |
| Regression | Predict outcomes | Continuous, normal |

### Statistical Significance
- p-value < 0.05: Statistically significant
- Means: <5% chance result is due to chance
- Does NOT mean: Practically important
- Always report: Effect size alongside significance

### Common Pitfalls
- Confusing correlation with causation
- Small sample size, large claims
- Multiple comparisons without correction
- Selection bias in sample
- Survivorship bias in analysis
```

### Phase 4: Usability Research

#### Usability Testing Protocol

```markdown
## Usability Test Guide

### Test Planning
| Element | Specification |
|---------|--------------|
| Objectives | What usability aspects to evaluate |
| Participants | 5-8 per round typical |
| Tasks | 4-6 realistic scenarios |
| Metrics | Success, time, errors, satisfaction |
| Method | Moderated vs. unmoderated |
| Fidelity | Prototype vs. live product |

### Task Design
Good tasks:
- Realistic scenarios users actually do
- Clear goal without prescribing path
- Measurable completion
- Appropriate difficulty range

Example:
"You want to invite a colleague to collaborate on a project. Please show me how you would do that."

NOT:
"Click the Share button and enter an email address."

### Moderation Script

#### Introduction (5 min)
"Thanks for participating. We're evaluating [product] to find ways to improve it. We're testing the product, not you - there are no wrong answers.

Please think aloud as you work - tell me what you're looking at, what you're thinking, and what you expect to happen.

I may not be able to help if you get stuck, because we want to see where people naturally struggle."

#### Tasks (30-45 min)
- Present scenario, not steps
- Observe without helping
- Ask "What are you thinking?"
- Note success, errors, time
- Rate confidence after each task

#### Debrief (10 min)
- Overall impressions
- Difficulty ranking
- Open suggestions
- Thank you

### Metrics to Collect
| Metric | Definition | Target |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Task success | Completed without help | >80% |
| Time on task | Duration to complete | Varies by task |
| Error rate | Mistakes per task | <3 |
| Satisfaction | Post-task rating (1-7) | >5 |
| SUS Score | System Usability Scale | >68 |
```

#### Qualitative Analysis

```markdown
## Qualitative Analysis Framework

### Affinity Diagramming
1. Write each observation on sticky note
2. Group related notes together
3. Name each group (theme)
4. Identify hierarchy and relationships
5. Document themes with evidence

### Coding Process
| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| Open coding | Label raw data segments |
| Axial coding | Group codes into categories |
| Selective coding | Identify core themes |
| Theory building | Connect themes to insights |

### Theme Documentation
| Theme | Description | Evidence Count | Key Quotes |
|-------|-------------|----------------|------------|
| [Theme name] | [What this means] | [N participants] | "[Quote]" |

### Insight Development
From observation → To insight:

Observation: "5 of 7 participants tried the Help menu first"
Insight: "Users expect Help to contain feature tutorials, not just documentation"

Observation → Pattern → Inference → Insight → Implication
```

## Common Pitfalls

### Research Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confirmation Bias**
   - Problem: Designing to prove hypothesis
   - Impact: Invalid findings
   - *Mitigation*: Seek disconfirming evidence, diverse researchers

2. **Leading Questions**
   - Problem: Suggesting "right" answer
   - Impact: Biased data
   - *Mitigation*: Neutral wording, pilot testing

3. **Small Sample Generalization**
   - Problem: Overstating findings from few participants
   - Impact: Misleading conclusions
   - *Mitigation*: State confidence levels, appropriate claims

4. **Selection Bias**
   - Problem: Unrepresentative participants
   - Impact: Results don't generalize
   - *Mitigation*: Careful screening, quota sampling

5. **Researcher Influence**
   - Problem: Body language or tone affects responses
   - Impact: Social desirability bias
   - *Mitigation*: Standardized protocols, blind analysis

6. **Analysis Without Framework**
   - Problem: Cherry-picking interesting quotes
   - Impact: Subjective interpretation
   - *Mitigation*: Systematic coding, inter-rater reliability

7. **Reporting Only Positive Findings**
   - Problem: Hiding inconvenient results
   - Impact: Incomplete picture
   - *Mitigation*: Report all findings, including surprises
