# Market Analysis

## Overview

Market analysis is the systematic examination of market dynamics, competitive forces, and growth opportunities to inform strategic decision-making. This skill enables organizations to quantify addressable opportunities, understand competitive positioning, and anticipate market shifts before they occur.

Effective market analysis transforms qualitative intuition into quantitative insights, providing the foundation for investment decisions, go-to-market strategies, and product development priorities. It bridges external market realities with internal strategic planning.

### Core Components

1. **Market Sizing**: TAM, SAM, SOM quantification
2. **Market Segmentation**: Customer and use case categorization
3. **Competitive Analysis**: Landscape mapping and positioning
4. **Trend Identification**: Macro and micro trend analysis
5. **Growth Forecasting**: Market trajectory projection
6. **Opportunity Assessment**: White space and gap analysis

## When to Use

### Primary Scenarios

- **New Market Entry**: Evaluating expansion into new geographies or verticals
- **Product Launch Planning**: Sizing opportunity for new offerings
- **Investment Decisions**: Supporting fundraising or M&A analysis
- **Strategic Planning**: Annual planning market context
- **Competitive Response**: Reacting to competitive moves
- **Pricing Strategy**: Understanding willingness to pay

### Trigger Indicators

- Leadership asking "How big is our market?"
- Investor questions about market opportunity
- Competitive dynamics shifting significantly
- New technology creating market disruption
- Customer needs evolving rapidly
- Expansion decisions requiring justification

## Core Processes

### Phase 1: Market Sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM)

#### Understanding the Framework

```markdown
## Market Sizing Definitions

### Total Addressable Market (TAM)
The entire revenue opportunity if you achieved 100% market
share. Represents the theoretical maximum.

### Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM)
The portion of TAM your product/service can realistically
serve given current capabilities, geography, and focus.

### Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM)
The realistic market share you can capture in the near
term (1-3 years) given competition and resources.
```

#### Top-Down Sizing Methodology

```markdown
## Top-Down Approach

### Step 1: Start with Industry Data
- Industry analyst reports (Gartner, Forrester, IDC)
- Government statistics and trade associations
- Public company filings and investor presentations

### Step 2: Apply Relevant Filters
- Geographic scope limitations
- Customer segment focus
- Product category boundaries

### Step 3: Calculate Market Values
TAM = Total Industry Size x Relevant Product Category %
SAM = TAM x Serviceable Geography % x Target Segment %
SOM = SAM x Realistic Market Share %

### Example Calculation
Industry Size: $50B
Product Category: 40% = $20B TAM
Serviceable Region: 60% x Target Segment: 50% = $6B SAM
Realistic Share: 5% = $300M SOM
```

#### Bottom-Up Sizing Methodology

```markdown
## Bottom-Up Approach

### Step 1: Define Unit Economics
- Average contract value (ACV)
- Pricing tiers and distribution
- Customer lifetime value

### Step 2: Count Potential Customers
- Total companies in target segments
- Employees or users within those companies
- Penetration rate assumptions

### Step 3: Calculate Market Values
TAM = Total Potential Customers x ACV
SAM = Qualified Customers x ACV
SOM = Acquirable Customers (1-3 yr) x ACV

### Example Calculation
Total SMBs in target vertical: 500,000
Average ACV: $5,000
TAM = 500,000 x $5,000 = $2.5B
Qualified (meet ICP): 40% = $1B SAM
Acquirable (3 years): 2% = $50M SOM
```

#### Triangulation Best Practices

Always validate sizing using multiple approaches:

1. Compare top-down and bottom-up estimates
2. Cross-reference with competitor revenues
3. Validate with customer research data
4. Adjust for market growth rates
5. Document assumptions clearly

### Phase 2: Competitive Landscape Mapping

#### Competitor Identification Framework

```markdown
## Competitor Categories

### Direct Competitors
- Same product, same market
- Head-to-head competitive deals
- Similar value propositions

### Indirect Competitors
- Different product, same problem
- Alternative solutions customers consider
- Adjacent market players

### Potential Competitors
- Companies that could enter
- Upstream/downstream players
- Technology platform companies

### Substitutes
- Non-software alternatives
- Manual processes
- In-house solutions
```


## Common Pitfalls

### Analysis Failures to Avoid

1. **Optimistic Sizing**: Inflating TAM with unrealistic assumptions
   - *Mitigation*: Use multiple methodologies, conservative assumptions

2. **Outdated Data**: Relying on stale market research
   - *Mitigation*: Check data freshness, supplement with primary research

3. **Competitor Blind Spots**: Missing indirect or emerging competitors
   - *Mitigation*: Broaden competitor definition, monitor adjacencies

4. **Confirmation Bias**: Finding data that supports preferred narrative
   - *Mitigation*: Seek disconfirming evidence, external review

5. **Analysis Overload**: Too much research, too little insight
   - *Mitigation*: Start with hypotheses, focused data gathering

6. **Static View**: Treating market as fixed rather than dynamic
   - *Mitigation*: Build in growth rates, scenario planning

7. **Vanity Sizing**: Quoting TAM when SOM is what matters
   - *Mitigation*: Lead with realistic obtainable market

### Warning Signs

- Market size that sounds too good to be true
- Unable to name specific target customers
- Competitive analysis that only shows weaknesses
- Trends identified but no strategic response
- Sizing doesn't match actual sales pipeline
