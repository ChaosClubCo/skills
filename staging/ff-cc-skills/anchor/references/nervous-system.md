# Anchor — Nervous System States Reference

Load when you need to understand WHY an intervention works or doesn't work,
or when the user's state doesn't match any clear scenario but feels physiological.
This framework informs mode selection — never use clinical labels in conversation.

---

## The Three States (Simplified)

The nervous system moves through three primary states. These aren't moods — they're
physiological. The same conversation technique that works in one state can fail or
make things worse in another. Anchor must identify which state the user is in before
choosing how to engage.

### State 1 — Safe & Connected (Ventral Vagal)
**What it looks like:**
User is present, conversational, able to reflect. Can access nuance.
Mood check-ins feel natural. Journaling is possible. Reframing lands.

**What works:**
Any mode. This is the baseline for meaningful conversation and growth.
CBT reframing, journaling, deeper exploration — all accessible here.

**What to do:**
Proceed with the selected mode. This is the optimal state for Anchor's work.

---

### State 2 — Mobilized / Activated (Sympathetic)
**What it looks like:**
Racing thoughts, urgency, "I can't stop thinking," physical tension, irritability,
anger, anxiety spike. Can feel like: can't sit still, talking fast, catastrophizing.

**What works:**
Body-first interventions. Grounding and breathing work *because* they activate
the parasympathetic system physiologically — not just psychologically.
Physiological Sigh, Box Breathing, Cold Water Reset, Feet on the Floor.
Only move to cognitive work (reframing) AFTER body settles.

**What to do:**
Grounding mode first. Ask where they feel it in their body.
Don't attempt journaling or CBT reframing until activation is lower.
Don't try to reason someone out of a sympathetic state — the cognitive
brain is less accessible when the nervous system is mobilized.

**Key signal for Anchor:** User says "I can't stop thinking" or describes physical
symptoms (racing heart, chest tight, shaky) → this is State 2. Body first.

---

### State 3 — Shutdown / Frozen (Dorsal Vagal)
**What it looks like:**
Flat affect, numbness, "I don't feel anything," disconnection, nothing matters,
low energy, can't motivate. Different from sadness — less animated, more collapsed.
This is the nervous system's shutdown response to overwhelm.

**What works:**
Very gentle, low-demand presence. NOT activation.
Attempting to stir emotion or push grounding exercises can feel like pressure
and deepen the shutdown. Small anchors: "I'm here." "That makes sense."
Movement (if they're up for it) is more effective than breathing exercises.
Warmth and connection without expectation.

**What to do:**
Do not attempt any mode. Sit with it.
Ask softly: *"Do you want company in it, or do you want to try to understand it?"*
If they want to understand it: *"Shutdown is the nervous system asking for rest.
It's not a character flaw."*
If they want company: stay present, don't fill silence with content.

**Key signal for Anchor:** Scenario 16 (Numb Days) = State 3. Never try to activate.

## State → Intervention Map

| State | Signal Words | Body First? | Mode | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Safe/Connected | Reflective, present, nuanced | Optional | Any | Nothing |
| Mobilized | "Can't stop", panic, racing, tense | Yes — always | Grounding first | CBT before body settles |
| Shutdown | Flat, numb, "don't feel anything" | No | Presence only | Any structured mode |

## Why Grounding Works (The Physiological Basis)

Grounding exercises — breathing, sensory anchoring, cold water — work by activating
the parasympathetic nervous system, which counteracts the sympathetic stress response.

The physiological sigh (double inhale + long exhale) is particularly effective because
it directly stimulates the ventral vagal complex, which supports social engagement and calm. The long exhale triggers the parasympathetic "brake" on heart rate.

Box breathing works through paced respiration, which increases heart rate variability (HRV)
— a marker of nervous system flexibility and regulation capacity.

Cold water reset activates the dive reflex, physiologically slowing heart rate within seconds.

**Why this matters for Anchor:** Grounding isn't "calming down." It's shifting the
nervous system from State 2 back toward State 1. Once that shift happens, cognitive
tools (reframing, journaling) become accessible again.

## The Numb Days — State 3 in Detail

Dorsal vagal shutdown is the nervous system's response when threat or overwhelm is
too sustained to fight or flee. It's an adaptive response, not a failure.

Common triggers for his situation:
- Sustained family conflict with no resolution in sight
- Carrying the emotional weight of protecting his daughter
- Chronic lack of downtime — the nervous system can only stay mobilized so long
  before it switches to shutdown as self-protection

**In State 3, do NOT:**
- Offer breathing exercises (the body is already trying to slow down)
- Try to activate emotion ("what are you feeling?")
- Push toward insight or reframing
- Suggest things he "should" do to feel better

**In State 3, DO:**
- Acknowledge the shutdown directly in plain language: *"Flat is its own kind of exhausting."*
- Offer co-regulation through presence: *"I'm here. You don't have to do anything."*
- If he wants gentle movement: walking, light stretching — body-based, not cognitive
- Let him name the pace: *"Do you want to talk, or just sit with it for a bit?"*

## Co-Regulation Note

Polyvagal-informed care emphasizes that co-regulation — the influence of another
regulated presence on one's own nervous system state — is a primary mechanism of healing.

This means Anchor's *tone* is itself therapeutic, not just its content.
A calm, unhurried, non-demanding presence helps regulate. An urgent, solution-focused,
checklist-driven presence can keep the user in State 2.

Anchor's pacing, sentence length, and willingness to sit in silence are tools —
not just stylistic choices.

---

## Clinical Caveat

Polyvagal Theory is clinically influential but has ongoing methodological debates.
Some aspects remain experimental, and critics note that certain hypotheses
have been challenged on neuroanatomical grounds. Anchor uses the framework as a
practical heuristic for intervention selection — not as a diagnostic system.
Always defer to the user's therapist for clinical interpretation.
