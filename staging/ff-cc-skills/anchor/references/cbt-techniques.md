# Anchor — CBT Technique Reference

Load when Reframe mode needs a specific technique for a thought pattern.
Use the thought pattern table to identify what's happening, then apply the technique.
Never use clinical labels in conversation — describe the technique in plain language.

---

## THOUGHT PATTERN → TECHNIQUE MAP

| Pattern | Sounds Like | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Catastrophizing | "This will ruin everything" | Decatastrophize, Best/Worst/Likely |
| All-or-nothing | "I always / I never" | Spectrum/Continuum |
| Mind reading | "They must think I'm an idiot" | Evidence Check, Perspective Shift |
| Fortune telling | "I know it's going to go badly" | Probability Estimate |
| Personalization | "This is my fault" | Responsibility Pie |

| Filtering | Only seeing negatives | Evidence For and Against |

---

## STATE PREREQUISITE

CBT techniques require State 1 (Safe/Connected) nervous system access to work.
If the user is in State 2 (activated/spiraling), run Grounding mode first.
If the user is in State 3 (shutdown/numb), do not attempt any technique.
Read `nervous-system.md` if unsure which state the user is in.

## TECHNIQUES

### Behavioral Activation
*For: low mood, avoidance, "I haven't done anything", withdrawal from things that used to help*
*Note: Most efficacious CBT technique for mood in digital delivery (Nature Medicine, 2025)*

The premise: behavior drives mood more than mood drives behavior. When low, people withdraw
from activities — which deepens the low. Small, concrete actions break the loop.

> "What's one small thing that usually makes you feel slightly better — even if just a little?"
> "Not a big project. Something small. Could be a 10-minute walk, a cup of coffee outside, texting someone."
> "You don't have to feel like doing it first. The feeling usually follows the action, not the other way around."

**Key distinction from pushing:** Never prescribe the activity. Ask what has worked *for him*.
Recovery habits from the Profile Card are a direct input here.

If he can't name anything: *"What did you used to do that helped, even if it feels far away right now?"*

### Evidence Check
*For: beliefs stated as facts, mind-reading*
> "What's the evidence this is true?"
> "What's the evidence it might not be?"
> "If a friend said this about themselves, what would you say?"
Don't push for counter-evidence — ask the first question and wait. They usually find it.

### Probability Estimate
*For: catastrophizing, fortune-telling*
> "0 is definitely won't happen, 10 is definitely will — where does this actually land?"
> "What would make it a 5 instead of a 9?"

### Best / Worst / Most Likely
*For: worst-case spiraling*
> "What's the worst realistic version?"
> "What's the best realistic version?"
> "What's actually most likely?"
The middle answer usually reveals the distortion. Don't rush to it.

### Decatastrophize
*For: "this will ruin everything"*
> "Say the worst version happened. What would that actually mean, practically?"
> "What would you do? Who would you talk to?"
Not about minimizing — about recognizing his own resilience.

### Spectrum / Continuum
*For: all-or-nothing*
> "You said 'always'. Can you think of one time it went okay — even just okay?"
> "On 0–100, where would today actually land?"

### Perspective Shift
*For: shame, fear of judgment, harsh self-view*
> "What would you tell a close friend thinking this about themselves?"
> "What would someone who loves you say about this situation?"
> "Five years from now — how will you see this?"

### Responsibility Pie
*For: over-personalization, excessive blame*
100% of responsibility needs to go somewhere. Walk through: environment, timing, other
people, context — then his part. Usually much smaller than he assigned himself.

### Separate Feeling from Fact
*For: "I feel it therefore it must be true"*
> "What are you feeling?"
> "What's the thought connected to that feeling?"
> "Is the feeling evidence the thought is true — or is it just a feeling?"
> "You can feel scared without the scary thing being certain."

### Compassionate Reframe
*For: self-criticism, "should" statements*
> "The way you're talking to yourself — would you say that to someone you care about?"
> "What would a kinder version of that thought sound like?"
> "What do you actually need to hear right now?"

## HARD RULES

- Validate before any examination. Always.
- Never argue with a thought. Examine it with curiosity.
- Never silver-line ("but look at the positives!")
- One technique per session. Don't chain them.
- End with open space, not a packaged resolution:
  > "You don't have to fix this today. Does having named it feel any different?"
